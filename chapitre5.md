# Chapter 5 - Knowledge in the Nineteenth and Early Twentieth Centuries

## Introduction

The nineteenth century and the beginning of the twentieth century intensified the transformation of knowledge. Industrialization, colonial expansion, new sciences, and new philosophies all altered the conditions under which reality was understood. The period did not produce a single theory of knowledge, but a proliferation of competing frameworks.

If the Scientific Revolution established the prestige of method, the nineteenth century multiplies the domains to which knowledge is applied and the scales on which it operates. Society, language, labor, life, history, consciousness, mathematics, and political economy all become objects of systematic reflection. The result is not greater simplicity, but a much richer and more conflictual epistemic landscape.

## Historical Context: Industry, Empire, and Social Transformation

Industrial society changed both the production of goods and the production of knowledge. Technical expertise, specialization, and scientific education became increasingly central. At the same time, European imperial expansion exposed thinkers to other cultures while often imposing a hierarchy of civilizations.

Knowledge became more powerful, more institutionalized, and more socially consequential. Universities expanded, disciplines specialized, museums and archives multiplied, and statistics became increasingly important to administration and state power.

This context matters because epistemology is never detached from the social organization of learning. What a society chooses to measure, classify, teach, and fund shapes what it becomes capable of knowing.

## Kant and the Conditions of Possible Knowledge

Immanuel Kant attempted to reconcile rationalism and empiricism. His central insight was that the mind does not merely receive the world passively. It actively structures experience.

Space and time, in Kant's view, are forms of intuition through which experience becomes possible. The mind also organizes experience through categories such as causality, substance, and quantity.

The result is a crucial distinction:

- phenomena: reality as it appears to us
- noumena: things as they are in themselves

Human beings can know phenomena, but not reality in itself independently of the conditions of human cognition. Kant therefore places a structural limit on knowledge while also explaining how objective science is possible.

Kant's achievement is decisive because he avoids two extremes. Against naive empiricism, he shows that experience is not raw material simply entering the mind unchanged. Against ungrounded rationalism, he insists that concepts without intuition are empty. Knowledge requires both receptivity and synthesis.

He therefore transforms the very question of epistemology. The issue is no longer only whether the mind corresponds to the world. It is also what conditions make experience, objectivity, and science possible in the first place.

## Hegel and Historical Knowledge

Hegel rejected the idea of truth as something static. Knowledge, for him, develops historically through dialectical movement. Concepts generate contradictions, these contradictions produce conflict, and new syntheses emerge.

Truth is therefore not simply possessed. It unfolds over time. Knowledge is both historical and collective, shaped through the development of spirit, culture, institutions, and conflict.

This view turns epistemology toward history. To understand truth is to understand processes rather than fixed positions. Error and contradiction are not merely failures; they are moments within development.

Hegel matters because he teaches later thought to see consciousness itself as historically formed. The mind does not arrive at truth outside of time. It moves through social forms, symbolic worlds, and conflicts that reshape what can be thought.

## Marx: Knowledge, Matter, and Social Position

Marx reworked Hegel's dialectic in materialist terms. The basic drivers of history are not abstract ideas but material conditions, labor, class relations, and economic structures.

This has a major epistemological implication: what people think is influenced by where they stand in social relations. Ideology is not merely false belief. It is a structured distortion linked to social interests.

Knowledge is therefore never entirely detached from power, labor, and class position.

Marx's contribution to epistemology is profound because he makes suspicion socially concrete. Error is not simply an accident of individual reasoning. It can be built into institutions and social arrangements. A society may misunderstand itself because its dominant concepts are serving dominant interests.

This insight later feeds ideology critique, sociology of knowledge, and many analyses of media, class, and political power.

## Positivism and the Scientific Ordering of Knowledge

Auguste Comte argued that human thought evolves through three stages:

- theological
- metaphysical
- positive or scientific

In the positive stage, knowledge no longer seeks ultimate causes or hidden essences. It looks for laws governing observable phenomena. Comte also proposed a hierarchy of sciences and helped define sociology as a scientific field.

Positivism reinforced the prestige of scientific method and encouraged the belief that society itself could be studied and managed scientifically.

Its attraction is easy to understand. If nature can be explained through laws and regularities, why should human societies not be approached in the same way? Positivism promises order, predictability, and rational administration.

Yet its weakness is equally visible. The more human life is reduced to what can be measured, the more meaning, conflict, interpretation, and lived experience risk being flattened. This tension between measurable knowledge and human significance remains central today.

## Pragmatism: Truth and Use

American pragmatists such as Charles Sanders Peirce, William James, and John Dewey reoriented the discussion by asking what truth does rather than what truth is in isolation.

For pragmatism, the value of an idea lies in its practical consequences. A belief is tested through inquiry, action, and problem-solving. Knowledge becomes experimental in a broad sense: it is validated through use, correction, and adaptation.

This approach avoids both rigid dogmatism and abstract skepticism. It presents inquiry as a living process tied to practice.

Pragmatism is especially important in democratic societies because it links knowledge to collective problem-solving. Inquiry becomes experimental not only in laboratories, but in education, politics, and social reform. Dewey in particular treats democracy itself as an epistemic form, a way of organizing public intelligence.

## Phenomenology and the Return to Experience

In Europe, phenomenology developed a different response to the limits of positivism. Edmund Husserl argued that philosophy must return to experience as it is lived before theoretical abstraction.

Later thinkers such as Heidegger, Sartre, and Merleau-Ponty deepened this move by showing that knowledge is always situated:

- in consciousness
- in existence
- in the body
- in the world of action and meaning

Phenomenology therefore challenges the fantasy of a purely detached observer. To know is always to know from somewhere.

This is one of the most important corrections to modern scientism. Objective knowledge is valuable, but it does not exhaust experience. Human beings do not encounter the world first as detached measuring machines. They encounter it as embodied, oriented, meaning-making beings.

## Logic, Language, and the Foundations of Knowledge

At the same time, another major development took place in logic and the philosophy of language.

Frege created modern symbolic logic, making reasoning more precise and formally analyzable. Russell sought to ground mathematics in logic. Wittgenstein explored the relation between language and world, first through formal structure and later through the diversity of language-games.

These developments matter because they show that knowledge depends not only on experience and reason, but also on the forms through which propositions are expressed. If language is confused, reasoning may be confused. If propositions can be formalized more clearly, hidden assumptions and invalid inferences become easier to detect.

This gives rise to a powerful modern hope: some philosophical problems may be clarified, or even dissolved, by greater linguistic and logical precision.

## Godel and the Limits of Formal Systems

Kurt Godel's incompleteness theorems dealt a decisive blow to the dream of complete formal certainty. In any sufficiently rich formal system, some truths cannot be proven within the system itself.

This result has deep philosophical force. Even mathematics, often treated as the paradigm of certainty, contains limits from within. Complete closure is impossible.

Godel therefore marks a sobering moment in the history of knowledge. The modern desire to secure all truth within a perfectly complete formal architecture runs into a limit that is not external but internal.

## Darwin, Freud, and the Decentering of the Human Subject

Two major intellectual shocks reshaped modern self-understanding.

Darwin showed that human beings are part of natural history rather than fixed creations outside it. Freud argued that consciousness is not master in its own house, since unconscious forces shape thought and behavior.

Together, these theories weakened older images of human exceptionalism and transparent self-knowledge. Human beings became more historical, more biological, and more psychologically opaque than many earlier frameworks had assumed.

This transformed epistemology because the knower could no longer be imagined as a simple rational subject standing transparently before the world. The subject itself had to be rethought.

## Marie Curie and Invisible Reality

The discovery of radioactivity revealed that unseen processes could transform matter from within. This changed the idea of what counts as real. Reality could no longer be identified only with what was directly visible or intuitively graspable.

Scientific instrumentation became an extension of perception. Knowledge increasingly depended on devices capable of revealing hidden structures of the world.

This point is decisive for modern science as a whole. A growing proportion of reality becomes accessible only through models, instruments, traces, and indirect effects. Knowledge becomes less intuitive but often more exact.

## Nietzsche and the Critique of Truth

Nietzsche challenged the idea of truth as neutral correspondence. He argued that truths are often stabilized interpretations serving forms of life, power, and value.

This does not simply abolish truth. It forces epistemology to confront the role of interpretation, perspective, and force in the production of what counts as valid knowledge.

Nietzsche is therefore a major precursor of later genealogical and critical traditions. He asks not only whether a claim is true, but what kind of life needs this claim, what type of force sustains it, and what it does in the world.

## Conclusion

The nineteenth and early twentieth centuries multiplied the dimensions of epistemology. Knowledge became historical with Hegel, social with Marx, practical with pragmatism, embodied with phenomenology, formalized through logic, destabilized by Godel, and decentered by Darwin and Freud.

This period did not simplify the problem of truth. It made it deeper, wider, and more difficult to separate from human life as a whole. The knower became historical, social, embodied, interested, and cognitively limited. Knowledge itself became more powerful, but also more reflexive, because it had to account for the conditions under which it was produced.
