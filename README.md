# Knowledge and Epistemology Book Project

This repository contains the source material and generated outputs for a short book on knowledge and epistemology. The project begins from six French source transcripts and turns them into an English course-style manuscript organized into chapters, an introduction, and a conclusion. Its main goal is to provide a clear, readable intellectual journey through the history of ideas about knowledge: how human beings distinguish truth from opinion, how reason and experience interact, why science became authoritative, and how modern societies struggle with uncertainty, bias, and manipulation.

The book is primarily centered on the European history of knowledge. Its main narrative follows the path from Greek Antiquity to medieval and Renaissance Europe, then through the Scientific Revolution, nineteenth-century philosophy, and the twentieth-century crisis of certainty. Alongside that core trajectory, the project includes a comparative chapter on non-Western traditions, including Indian, Buddhist, Chinese, Islamic, African, and Indigenous approaches to knowledge. That chapter is meant to widen perspective and clarify the limits of a purely European story, while remaining more selective than the main historical line.

The repository includes Markdown source files for each chapter, a Python script that assembles them into a styled HTML book and a PDF version, and the generated outputs themselves. The introduction explains why epistemology matters today, especially in a world saturated with information but not always with clarity. The chapters then explore the major stages of epistemological thought: the birth of rational inquiry, the role of revelation and institutions, the rise of science and method, the historical and social critique of knowledge, and the modern awareness that truth is shaped by cognition, institutions, language, and power.

In short, this project is both a historical overview and a practical reflection on what it means to know responsibly.
